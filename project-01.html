Overview

Executive Summary

Built a user behavior analytics infrastructure from the ground up for a digital entertainment platform, delivering a framework spanning user tagging, dynamic behavioral profiling, and multi-dimensional segmentation. The system applies feature engineering to extract behavioral signals and unsupervised clustering to identify distinct user cohorts — providing a shared, data-driven profiling foundation for risk, product, and business decision-making.

This project focuses on deep, long-horizon behavioral pattern discovery, using batch analysis to produce prioritized anomaly lists as the basis for manual risk review.

Problem

Business Background

The platform lacked a proactive behavioral detection model: existing mechanisms relied on passive, rule-based alerts that struggled with complex behavioral patterns, generating high false-positive rates that fatigued the risk operations team and buried genuine anomalies.

There was also no baseline infrastructure for players or games, making it impossible to quantify how far any behavior deviated from normal — leaving the risk team unable to proactively surface emerging risks.

Solution

Framework Architecture

The framework consists of five modules, each targeting a distinct dimension of behavioral risk:

  • Tags & Baselines (Module 0): Player segmentation via activity, heat, new-player, and volatility tags; game × currency baselines with Winsorization for robust outlier handling
  • Player Behavior Detection (Module 1): Weighted scoring (short-term 60%, long-term 20%, benchmark comparison 20%) with Z-Score deviation quantification per player
  • Player Group Detection (Module 1b): PCA/SVD dimensionality reduction followed by KMeans and MiniBatch KMeans clustering to surface cohorts with anomalous behavioral fingerprints
  • Game System Detection (Module 2): Monitors 10-minute volatility, daily stability index, and baseline deviation to catch game-level systemic risk signals
  • Player–Game Interaction (Module 3): Cross-analysis of player anomaly co-occurrence with specific games to detect coordinated behavioral patterns

Results

Key Insights & Business Impact

The framework integrates over a year of daily transaction data (2024/09–2025/08) and two months of 10-minute granularity data across multiple currencies and game types. Five modules produce four standardized output types — player anomalies, cluster anomalies, game anomalies, and interaction anomalies — feeding directly into compliance workflows without manual reformatting.

The framework surfaced three distinct case types in production:

  • Interaction anomaly: multiple accounts concentrated on a single game
  • Individual anomaly: significant deviation from a player's own historical behavior
  • Group anomaly (Cluster 18): synchronized concentration, pacing, and win rate

All cases are filtered by P99 percentile thresholding, ensuring only the top 1% highest-risk signals enter the manual review queue.

Since deployment, every case that entered the manual review queue has been confirmed by the risk team as a genuine anomaly, with zero false positives identified to date.

Reflection

Challenges & Trade-offs

The core challenge was balancing sensitivity against false positives: Z-Score and P99 thresholds converged iteratively through alert volume vs. manual review feedback, rather than being set upfront.

A second trade-off was clustering method selection. Given the scale of the player base, full KMeans recomputation on every daily batch would scale compute cost linearly. Switching to MiniBatch KMeans — updating centroids from small sampled batches — kept the daily schedule within a fixed time window, at the cost of some cluster boundary precision. For the purpose of surfacing outlier cohorts, that minor boundary blur didn't compromise detection effectiveness.

Fewer False Alarms, Better UX: How Multi-condition Risk Scoring Replaced Single-Threshold Alerts

Overview

Executive Summary

Redesigned a risk alert system for a digital entertainment platform, replacing single-threshold alerts with a multi-condition risk scoring model. The new model integrates machine learning predictions, game performance metrics, and user profile signals across three dimensions, dynamically adjusting trigger logic by score.

This project focuses on calibrating the sensitivity of the existing alert system itself — determining trigger thresholds so that different risk tiers receive differentiated scrutiny.

Problem

Business Background

The existing risk control applied uniform fixed thresholds across all user profiles, resulting in excessive unnecessary suspensions for low-risk users — damaging user experience and platform revenue. Every triggered alert required a manual ticket to be opened and investigated regardless of actual risk level, consuming significant review time and operational capacity.

Resources were not concentrated where they mattered, so genuinely high-risk events received insufficient attention.

Solution

Multi-dimensional Scoring Model

The scoring system comprises three dimensions that together reflect a user's overall risk level:

  • Machine Learning dimension: Incorporates model predictions as a risk prior, reflecting the system-level risk assessment for the user
  • Game Performance dimension: Quantifies deviation in RTP, win rate, and hit rate relative to baselines using statistical scoring methods
  • User Profile dimension: Adjusts individual risk baselines based on contextual factors such as currency and service provider

The combined score classifies users into low, medium, and high risk tiers, with thresholds adjusted accordingly: low-risk users see thresholds relaxed to reduce false-positive noise; high-risk users are reviewed at the original threshold; critical-risk cases are escalated directly to manual review.

Implemented in two phases: simulation on historical data to validate outcomes, followed by live deployment to reduce go-live risk.

Results

Key Insights & Business Impact

The system operates across two trigger mechanisms — alerts (notifications) and limits (auto-suspension on threshold breach). Post-deployment data shows three measurable improvements: the multi-condition risk model replaces single-threshold alerts, cutting per-case review time by roughly 50%; the auto-suspend mechanism replaces manual limit adjustments, reducing unnecessary automatic game suspensions by 80% — exceeding the original ≥50% target — while substantially cutting the manual intervention burden on risk operators; ticket volume drops 72%, allowing risk operators to focus exclusively on cases requiring genuine action — improving both user experience and operational efficiency simultaneously.

Reflection

Challenges & Trade-offs

The core trade-off was framing recall vs. precision in business terms: over-optimizing for recall keeps the false-positive burden high, continually burying risk operators in low-risk cases; over-optimizing for precision risks missing genuine threats, particularly novel anomaly patterns that sit in ambiguous territory early on.

A single global threshold couldn't serve both ends at once. The scoring tiers were designed to let each risk segment apply its own threshold looseness — relaxed for low-risk users to cut unnecessary friction, held at full sensitivity for high-risk users — structurally decomposing the "one threshold for everyone" tension into multiple segment-specific strategies.
